Star Life Cycles

Stars change over millions to billions of years.

A star’s fate depends mainly on its mass. Small and medium stars age gently, while the most massive stars end in dramatic collapses and explosions that seed space with heavier elements.

Protostar

Gravity pulls cold gas and dust together inside a nebula. The growing core heats up as the future star forms.

→

Main sequence star

Hydrogen fusion begins. For most of a star’s life, inward gravity and outward pressure stay in balance.

Lower-mass pathway

Red giant

When core hydrogen runs low, the outer layers expand and cool, making the star larger and redder.

White dwarf

The outer layers drift away, leaving behind a hot, dense core that slowly cools over immense time.

Higher-mass pathway

Red supergiant

Massive stars fuse heavier elements in layered shells as they swell into enormous supergiants.

Supernova

When the core can no longer support itself, it collapses and the star can explode, briefly outshining whole galaxies.

Neutron star or black hole

The collapsed core may remain as an ultra-dense neutron star or, if massive enough, become a black hole.

Why this matters

Stellar lives shape the cosmos.

Stars forge many of the elements needed for planets and life. Their winds and explosions enrich space, trigger new star formation, and help build the visible structure of galaxies.

Guiding idea

Mass determines the story: the bigger the star, the faster and more dramatic the ending.
